Contemporary China No. 3: 清明节 (qīngmíng jié)

posted on 07 Apr 2012 by Alex

Instead of having a Chinese culture class, Alex and Rocky are having adventures in and around Beijing. "Contemporary China" is a series of blog posts that takes a look at our experiences with Chinese culture.

This is Alex here again with a blog post about a recent Chinese holiday that just took place. It is one of China's many ghost holidays the occur throughout the year and it is very important to many Chinese and their families. I am speaking about the holiday known as 清明节 (qīngmíng jié).

清明节, or Tomb Sweeping Festival, is a time for many Chinese to go and visit the graves of their ancestors and departed loved ones and honor them by cleaning their tombstones. 清明节 is also one of the few Chinese festivals that is based on a solar calendar rather than on the lunar calendar. The festival usually starts on April 2, but the actual day to visit graves is usually on April 5. That the festival fell on April 4 this year is quite interesting. In Chinese, the word for four is 四 (sì). The word for death is 死 (sǐ). As the sounds are very similar, the number four gets a bad wrap and is widely considered unlucky. April is the fourth month of the year, so this year, the holiday that is concerned with the dead happened on 4/4, a day that sounds very similar to death/death.

Being half-Chinese myself and interested in the Chinese culture, I truly wanted to fully participate in this holiday. But since I was stuck in Beijing and unable to visit my father's village in 广东 (guǎngdōng) I decided to do the next best thing and visit the graves that belong to the founders of my Kung Fu school instead. Because I felt if could not properly honor family, at least I would gain merit in honoring teachers.

The two main Kung Fu masters that I would be seeing are 董海川 (Dǒng Hǎichuān) and 李子鳴 (Lǐ Zǐmíng). They are two famous and crucial practitioners of the martial arts I practice known as 八卦掌 (Bāguàzhǎng). I was surprised to find that their graves were only 28 minutes from my school to the West and near the Summer place as well a cemetery known as 万安公墓 (Wàn ān gōngmù).

When I arrived to the entrance of the cemetery, it was bustling with activity; people were gathering at the entrance to buy flowers from the vendors as well as offerings for the dead. Aside from the obvious flowers and incense often given when visiting graves, the Chinese also have an array of paper offerings you can give to your ancestors who are in the afterlife. These usually include paper money or shoes and clothes for your ancestors to wear in the afterlife. In contemporary China, people have become more creative and modern with their paper offerings, sometimes giving their ancestors TVs, silverware, cars, cellphones, and more recently iPads for the loved ones to use in the afterlife. These more modern offerings are usually drawn on or made out of paper and are then burned. I should stress however that Wan An Cemetary does not allow you to do that. So with that in mind, I decided to buy a bouquet of flowers instead.

The sun was shining high and the sky was the deepest of blues as I entered Wan An I found the reception desk which was also busy with people and managed to get myself a map of the complex. I discovered that the graves of my Kung Fu masters were at the outermost edge of the cemetery with a nice close view of the mountains near Beijing. I was also surprised to find that there were already flowers and offerings laid out for both of the tombs and as I arrived. A large group of other people, both men and women, also arrived to make their offerings to Dong Haichuan and Li Ziming. I wisely decided not to take pictures of them or the grave site at this time as they were paying their respects and cleaning the tomb. I also did not know if they were family members or also fellow martial art students as the teachers I came to see died quite a long time ago (Dong Haichuan in 1882 and Li Ziming in 1993). I also helped out a little bit in cleaning and tending to the tombs by clearing away some plastic debris from the tombs as well as picking up and straightening previous offerings that had fallen or were blown over by the wind.

After paying my respects I decided to wander around the graveyard and observe what other people were doing to honor their ancestors. I saw many carrying buckets of water and straw brooms to both sweep and wash the tombs as well as laying food offerings on the tombstones for the departed. I saw one family in particular eating food themselves as laid down their offerings to the graves. An old man amongst the group with a half-eaten banana in his hand casually talking to the tombstones as the other family members cleaned the tomb and put down flowers. I do not know particularly what he was saying but the way he was talking it seemed he was keeping them up-to-speed with all of the current family affairs giving the feel of a family reunion to the reverent atmosphere. Wan An also had refreshments laid out around the cemetary for people visiting the cemetery that day.

All in all, the trip to Wan An Cemetary was interesting, giving insight into how the modern Chinese still hold on to some traditions of the past while adding a modern touch. Qing Ming Festival for many Chinese seems to be a time of remembrance and of family. A sentiment some may find cliché but a sentiment many people can relate to nonetheless.

Now if you'll excuse my I've got a paper flat screen TV I've got to burn for my grandma.
